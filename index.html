MLI is for people who want to move from being interested in AI to actually doing machine learning work.

A community with a research spine.

AI is moving quickly, but competence does not come from hype. It comes from reading, building, testing, failing, documenting, and improving.

Machine Learning Ilorin exists to create a serious local environment for that work: paper reading, datasets, notebooks, model evaluation, fine-tuning, reports, and practical experiments grounded in Nigerian realities. Ilorin is the base; Nigeria is the primary context.

What happens inside MLI

Every cycle should leave something useful behind: notes, data, code, prompts, model outputs, evaluation results, or a short report.

01

Read and reproduce

We study papers, model reports, technical guides, and reproducible workflows instead of chasing surface-level trends.

02

Run experiments

We design small, concrete experiments around prompts, datasets, baselines, open-source models, and evaluation rubrics.

03

Build for Nigerian context

We care about Nigerian English, Nigerian languages, education, commerce, public information, and low-resource settings across Nigeria.

Cycle 001: Local LLM Evaluation

The first research cycle starts with a practical question: how well do open-source language models understand Nigerian contexts?

First experiment

Test models against local prompts, then document what fails.

Members will run a 50-prompt local-context bank across selected open-source models, score responses for correctness, usefulness, cultural fit, clarity, hallucination risk, and safety, then write up the findings.

01
50-prompt Nigerian-context evaluation bank.
02
Raw model responses saved before scoring.
03
Evaluation sheet with rubric-based scoring.
04
Short report on strengths, failures, and next experiments.

How the monthly rhythm works

Small enough to start. Serious enough to compound.

Week 1

Research

Pick the theme, read one useful reference, and define the experiment.

Week 2

Lab

Set up prompts, datasets, notebooks, baselines, and shared tasks.

Week 3

Experiment

Run model outputs, score results, debug failures, and compare notes.

Week 4

Report

Demo findings, publish notes, and decide the next research question.

Who should join?

You do not need to be an expert. You do need to be active.

  • People willing to attend research and experiment sessions.
  • People interested in open-source models, datasets, and evaluation.
  • People who can contribute through research, code, data, documentation, infrastructure, or coordination.
  • People curious about Nigeria-context AI for Nigerian languages, education, commerce, public services, health, agriculture, and everyday problems.
